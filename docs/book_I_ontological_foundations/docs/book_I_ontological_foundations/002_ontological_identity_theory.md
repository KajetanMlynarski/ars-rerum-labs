## 1. The Primacy of Identity

The theory begins with a radical reorientation: we take the concept of **identity** as primary, not space, time, or matter. Two fundamental distinctions are introduced:

- **Identical objects** are those that are numerically the same in a given context — they can differ in identity (e.g., two identical spheres are identical as spheres but distinct as individuals).
- **The same object** is one that persists through change — it need not be identical at different times (e.g., a person at five and at thirty-five is the same person but not identical).

This distinction allows us to separate two fundamentally different modes of existence: **spatial existence** (being identical but distinct) and **temporal existence** (being the same through change).

---

## 2. Real Space: The Space of Identical Objects

Consider two identical spheres. They are distinguished by the distance between them — a relational property that belongs not to either sphere individually but to the **pair** as a composite object.

> **Principle 1: Space is a property of objects with composite identity.**

Distance is a measure of difference. Therefore, space is not a container or a background — it is a **relation between identical objects**. A single sphere, too, is a spatial object because it is composed of identical points, distinguished only by their positions relative to each other.

The fundamental properties of spatial objects are **extensiveness** and **multitude**. Real numbers describe distances in this space, which we call **real space** — the space of identical but identity-wise different objects.

---

## 3. Identity Space: The Space of the Same Objects

A temporal object — something that persists through change — is an object that is "the same" across different states. When a single object is two (or more) different states, we call it **endurance** (if unchanged) or **change** (if different). The difference between these states is a **distance of an object from itself**.

In real space, the smallest sensible distance is zero (a point from itself). But a temporal object has a non-zero distance from itself — it is different from what it was. Such distances cannot be measured by real numbers; they require **orthogonal numbers**. Imaginary numbers are the natural tool for describing **identity distances** (distances of an object "from itself").

> **Thus: Real numbers describe spatial distances; imaginary numbers describe temporal (identity) distances.**

The space of the same objects — where an object is described across its changes — is called **identity space**. Intervals in this space are measured by imaginary numbers. Composite space (spacetime) is the combination of real and imaginary coordinates, allowing us to describe objects that are both extended in space and persistent in time.

## 4. Theory of Fuzzy Identity

To formalize these intuitions, the paper introduces a **theory of fuzzy identity**, analogous to fuzzy set theory but with crucial differences.

| Concept | Symbol | Meaning |
|:--------|:-------|:--------|
| General object | A | A composite entity (e.g., a pair of spheres) |
| Elementary object | a, aₑₗ | An indivisible constituent (e.g., a point) |
| Identity relation | e | "A is a" (A is the elementary object a) |
| Degree of identity | SN ∈ [0,1] | The extent to which A is a |

**Key distinctions from set theory:**
- In set theory, a set is not its elements. In fuzzy identity theory, a general object **is**, to some degree, each of its elementary objects.
- The relation is **asymmetric**: the degree to which A is a may differ from the degree to which a is A.

**Fundamental relations:**
1. **Simple Identity:** `A e a` — A is the elementary object a.
2. **Fuzzy Identity:** `A e a SN n` — A is a to degree n.
3. **In-relation:** `A e a SN n in B` — A is a to degree n within the context of B.
4. **Rel-relation:** `A e a SN n rel C` — A is a to degree n with respect to identity C.

**Types of fuzziness:**

| Type | Condition | Meaning |
|:-----|:----------|:--------|
| Normal | Σ SN(A = a_i) = 1 | A is exactly and entirely its components |
| Degraded | Σ SN(A = a_i) < 1 | A is "less than itself" |
| Excessive | Σ SN(A = a_i) > 1 | A is "more than itself" |
| Uniform | SN(A = a_i) = 1/n, Σ = 1 | Equal identity with all components |

**Paradoxical identity** (excessive + components not identical) describes **temporal objects**. An object that is exactly two different things (e.g., entirely close and entirely far) is a temporal object — its "sequential development" is what we call time.

## 5. The Elementary Cell

The smallest temporal object (universe A) has exactly one elementary object. Its sequential development consists of two "moments" — it must be, to some extent, two different spatial objects that are, to some extent, the same object.

Key properties of the elementary cell:

- It has a constant value **P** (the degree of identity across the cell). For maximal continuity without violating identity, **P = 0.5** — the "condition of 0.5".
- It determines **elementary spatial distance** |el| and **elementary temporal distance** |cₑₗ|.
- It defines **elementary mass** |aₑₗ| — the Planck mass in natural units.

> **Principle 6:** There exists an elementary degree of identity |aₑₗ| = 1/n, where n is the number of elementary objects in A.

> **Principle 7:** Spatial and temporal distances corresponding to the elementary change of identity (0.5) are the smallest sensible distances in universe A.

> **Principle 8:** Every universe A is characterized by three elementary units: identity |aₑₗ|, spatial distance |el|, and temporal distance |cₑₗ|.

---

## 6. Motion and the Uncertainty Principle

From the structure of the elementary cell:

1. **Every elementary object is in motion** (Principle 10). Motion is the displacement of an object's identity in space while maintaining its identity.

2. **There exists an elementary speed** vₑₗ = |el|/|cₑₗ| — the speed of elementary objects relative to their own identity (Principle 12).

3. **The Heisenberg uncertainty principle emerges from the geometry of the cell.** An elementary object cannot be simultaneously localized in space and time with precision greater than the dimensions of the elementary cell. Forcing spatial precision "scatters" the temporal coordinate, and vice versa.

> **Principle 13:** Simultaneous precise determination of the spatial and temporal location of an elementary object is impossible and nonsensical in the spacetime of universe A.

---

## 7. The Emergence of Three Spatial Dimensions

When the universe A contains multiple elementary cells, their identity fields interact. The elementary object is fuzzy not only on its own cell but also, to the same degree, on segments reaching into neighboring cells. This creates a structure where the elementary object is represented by **three independently contemporary spatial vectors** (past, present, future).

These three vectors are orthogonal to each other and to the temporal vector. Therefore:

> **Principle 17:** The space of universe A is three-dimensional; spacetime is four-dimensional.

For distances smaller than three elementary lengths, dimensionality reduces (to 2D or 1D) — a prediction that resonates with the behavior of physical systems at very small scales.

---

## 8. The Speed Limit and Relativity

The "condition of 0.5" imposes a maximum speed: no object can move faster than the elementary speed vₑₗ. This speed is invariant across reference frames, leading to the Lorentz transformations:

$$x' = \frac{x - vt}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/v_{el}^2}}, \quad t' = \frac{t - (v/v_{el}^2)x}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/v_{el}^2}}$$

An object moving at this speed is "flat" — it lacks a spatial "future" vector. This is the theory's interpretation of light.

---

## 9. Distances and Identity Fields

Distance is defined as the inverse of identity: an identity degree of 0.5 corresponds to a distance of 2, 0.25 to 4, etc. Thus:

> **Identity decreases exponentially inversely to distance.**

The identity field of an object extends infinitely, but with decreasing strength. Composite objects (like massive bodies) have stronger identity fields due to the summation of identities from their elementary components. They act as "sources" of identity fields, with their own unit of distance |Zᵣₑₗ| > |el|.

The identity field of a composite object is not homogeneous. Distinguished objects appear at specific distances from its center:

$$2, 4, 6, 10, \dots \quad \text{or} \quad 2 + 2(2^{n-1})$$

This pattern is remarkably close to the **Titius-Bode law** of planetary distances — a prediction that the theory makes and that invites empirical testing.

---

## 10. Gravitation as Identity Interaction

Consider two objects Z and Z' with masses proportional to m and m' (their degrees of identity with A). At a distance n|el|, their identity fields interact. The increase in identity (the "force") is given by:

$$P = 2 \cdot \frac{m \cdot m'}{n^2 - n}$$

For large distances, this approximates:

$$P \approx 2 \cdot \frac{m \cdot m'}{n^2}$$

This is the inverse-square law of gravitation. The constant of proportionality — the gravitational constant G — emerges from the geometry of the elementary cell and the choice of units. In natural units (c = ℏ = 1), G is predicted to be 2.

---

## 11. Mass, Rotation, and Electrostatic Analog

The degree of identity with A is interpreted as **mass**. The elementary mass |aₑₗ| corresponds to the **Planck mass**:

$$m_p' = \sqrt{2c\hbar G^{-1}} \approx 3.08 \times 10^{-8} \text{ kg}$$

The smallest sensible mass (rest mass) is derived as:

$$m_{\min} \approx 1.48 \times 10^{-30} \text{ kg} \approx 1.62 \times m_e$$

This matches the electron mass to within a factor — a striking quantitative success.

Elementary objects rotate around their four-dimensional axes. Two objects rotating in opposite directions have a zone of velocity summation, producing a monopolar effect. This effect, interpreted as a relativistic increase in mass, yields a force that exactly matches the Coulomb force between elementary charges:

$$\left|\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{e^2}{r^2}\right| \approx G \frac{(m_p'')^2}{r^2}$$

where mₚ'' is the "effective Planck mass" derived from the volume of the elementary cell in real space. The numerical agreement is within 0.03% — a powerful indication that **electrostatics and gravitation are two manifestations of the same identity interaction**, distinguished only by the rotational state of the objects.

---

## 12. Fuzziness, Probability, and Quantum Mechanics

The degree of fuzziness SN is mathematically identical to probability. An object evenly fuzzy over a volume has equal probability of being located at any point within it. The identity distribution is a **probability distribution**.

Elementary objects are fuzzy in their own (complex) identity space. In real space, they are, to a certain degree (with some probability), "everywhere." They become localized only through interaction with other objects — an event that exceeds the Planck constant. This is the theory's account of **wavefunction collapse** and the measurement problem.

---

## 13. Predictions and Experimental Tests

The theory makes several testable predictions:

1. **Non-uniformity of gravitational fields:** The identity field of a massive object is not perfectly homogeneous; "distinguished objects" appear at specific distances. This predicts a deviation from pure Newtonian gravity, possibly observable in precision measurements.

2. **A planetary spacing law:** The pattern \(2 + 2(2^{n-1})\) closely matches the Titius-Bode law, suggesting that planetary formation is influenced by gravitational field non-uniformities.

3. **An additional force between rotating bodies:** The relativistic increase in mass for rotating objects should produce an extra force proportional to the rotational speed. While small, this effect is in principle measurable.

4. **The electron mass:** The predicted minimum mass is \(1.62 \times m_e\) — remarkably close to the actual electron mass, given the approximations used.

---

## 14. Summary: From Identity to Physics

The theory derives, from a single primitive concept (identity):

- **Space** as a relational property of identical objects
- **Time** as the sequential development of objects with paradoxical identity
- **Three spatial dimensions** and **four-dimensional spacetime**
- The **Lorentz transformations** and the **constancy of the speed of light**
- The **Heisenberg uncertainty principle**
- **Newtonian gravity** with an inverse-square law
- **Planck mass**, **electron mass**, and the **gravitational constant**
- A quantitative link between **gravitation and electrostatics**
- The **wavefunction** as an identity distribution and **collapse** as interaction

This is not a "theory of everything" in the conventional sense — it is something deeper: a **derivation of physics from the ontology of identity**. The constants of nature are not free parameters but **deduced consequences** of the structure of identity itself.
