# Ars Rerum et Imaginationis: Two Modes of Creation and Cognition

**Source:** Młynarski, K. (2025). *Ars Rerum et Imaginationis*. Preprint on RG.

---

## 1. The Question of Drawability

The world possesses certain properties that make it possible to draw things — or more generally, to represent objects. This is likely connected to the fact that objects have, in a broad sense, **shape**. Both physical objects and drawings have shape. This is especially evident in sculpture: one can sculpt a horse out of clay or stone, and the material will take on the same shape as the model horse. A drawing, of course, is flat, but it also reflects shape. Both the object being drawn and the drawing itself share corresponding shapes (though this correspondence is not as direct as in sculpture).

What can be drawn? This is a truly philosophical question. At any rate, one can draw, with great precision, all macroscopic things in the world — trees, rocks, bacteria, crystals. Engaging in such realistic, representational drawing may be considered a kind of **art of reality** (*ars rerum*).

Yet drawing can also express emotional states, depict nonexistent objects, or develop itself independently of any specific reference, in a purely formal (non-representational) manner — focusing solely on compositional qualities. Drawings such as maps often allow for prediction. This also applies to the drawing of imagined objects. Suppose a draftsman knows only animals that walk on two or four legs — he may easily invent and draw creatures with more legs, and it may later happen that such creatures are discovered. Drawing practiced in this way becomes an **art of imagination** (*ars imaginationis*).

## 2. Object-Free Activities

Drawing is an activity that has its own mode of practice, its techniques and methods, and at the same time is an **object-free activity**. That is: although it has a method (not everything we do qualifies as drawing), it does not have a designated subject matter: one can draw anything, or create drawings unrelated to anything that exists in the real world.

Many areas of art fall into this category of object-free activities. Literature, for example, represents the world by means of language: through names, structures, and grammatical constructions.

What can be described? Presumably a great deal. Is the world "literaturizable" (describable in language)? As in the case of drawing, we may answer: presumably to a very significant extent. Is the world literature? Only insofar as it contains literature.

Object-free activities are particularly useful in fields that **do** have a designated subject. Why? Precisely because they are object-free but possess refined and well-developed methods. They can therefore be employed when there is particular interest in a specific subject matter.

---

## 3. The Sciences as Object-Engaged Disciplines

Examples of important domains with designated subjects are the modern natural sciences: geology, geography, chemistry, biology, physics, and astronomy. Of course, the subjects of these sciences are not always precisely defined, but they are undoubtedly clearly indicated. For example, despite many efforts, we do not know whether physics has one subject or several, but it is certain that not every object of our activity belongs to the domain of physics.

Every monograph in the natural sciences is written in text — it therefore belongs to (broadly understood) literature. Practically every such work also contains at least a few drawings (maps or graphs), and thus makes use of the art of drawing. These texts and drawings may not be particularly interesting to those engaged in literary or visual arts, but they nonetheless belong to these domains.

Disciplines that are **object-engaged** place the highest value on the fidelity of representation (description, modeling), whereas in object-free disciplines, **aesthetic criteria** are of decisive importance.

## 4. Mathematics: Between Ars Rerum and Ars Imaginationis

We have overlooked something. Just as it is impossible to imagine a modern monograph without text and drawings, it is equally impossible for it not to contain at least one mathematical formula, even a very simple one. This formula may be of little interest to a mathematician but can be of great importance to a natural scientist. For instance, $E = mc^2$ — despite its popularity, it is genuinely the foundation of a significant part of physics, though a mathematician is unlikely to take much interest in it (even if he may not remain entirely indifferent to the elegance of its derivation).

The world (reality) possesses certain fundamental and highly general properties. The observation that shape can be represented in drawing or in clay offers immense possibilities for application — both with respect to "given reality" and in abstraction from it.

As *ars rerum* (the art of reality, "applied art"), drawing can present highly complex objects with remarkable precision — for instance, the structure of tissues. Why? Because in such cases, the drawing **conforms to the object**.

In a similar way, linear algebra finds excellent applications in physics because it arose as a representation of the fundamental principles of motion, particularly planetary motion (as in the differential calculus of Leibniz and Newton). It is interesting that many people are struck by the "extraordinary" applicability of mathematics (Wigner's "paradox"), but comparatively little attention is paid to the no less remarkable effectiveness of drawing or of literature.

## 5. Mathematics as Experimental Science

As *ars rerum*, drawing properly belongs to the domain of the natural sciences. It is an observational and experimental activity, producing intersubjectively communicable knowledge in the form of images. Of course, these experiments are extremely inexpensive compared to those of advanced particle physics. They are inexpensive because they concern the most fundamental and, at the same time, the most universal properties of the world — and precisely because they are universal, they cannot be hidden. Although they are not hidden, they nevertheless reach very deep.

**Mathematica rerum** is likewise an experimental natural science, whose investigations are considerably less costly than those undertaken by physicists. This idea was very clearly expressed by Vladimir Arnold. A branch or a piece of wire can be cut in such a way that, when we place their ends together, the opposite ends also align. We introduce a name: **halves**. And another: **two**. This is no less fundamental than the possession of shape. In this way we represent the most common and most fundamental properties of the world, and it is no wonder that we are able to reach far.

Mathematica rerum, or the mathematics of reality, is **applied mathematics** — used to describe objects in the world much like realistic drawing.

As *ars imaginationis*, it deals with arbitrarily abstract objects, those we are able to imagine (even if not necessarily represent visually, since abstraction exceeds simple visuality). By its nature, it is an **art of imagination**. It is object-free, and for this reason it can also be developed as mathematica rerum.

## 6. The Experimental Origins of Mathematics

Historically, mathematics — so far as we know — begins precisely as an experimental science. The Sumerians sometimes calculated areas by laying small stones over a shape and later attempted to find the appropriate methods of computation. Their formulas were thus originally "phenomenological formulas." Incidentally, the idea of experimental mathematics is now returning, except that instead of stones, we use computers — for instance, to check the possible convergence of series.

However, this experimental phase constitutes only the beginning. Soon, the possibility is recognized of operating with mathematical tools *in abstracto*. This is similar to drawing: from the observation that a given tool leaves a mark on a surface, one can proceed to more or less skillful representations of real objects (e.g., animals) and then of unreal ones (e.g., deities). One can draw figures with two, four, eight, or even a thousand arms. Likewise, in geometry, we may increase the number of spatial dimensions.

This is a feature of object-free activities: they are particularly suited to engaging with real objects, which often serve as our original inspiration. Yet in essence, for these fields, such real objects are simply among the possible ones — fundamentally the same as any others. These activities are therefore *par excellence* creative: they search and construct spaces of possible objects alike.

## 7. Philosophical Conceptions of Mathematics

Several philosophical conceptions attempt to define the subject matter of mathematics:

| Position | View |
|:---------|:-----|
| **Platonism (Idealism)** | Mathematical objects are "perfect," immutable ideas existing beyond space and time. An extreme Platonist was Georg Cantor. |
| **Realism** | Mathematical objects have real, sovereign existence, independent of our constructions. A common position among mathematicians. |
| **Intuitionism (Constructivism)** | Mathematical objects are constructions of our minds. They exist only insofar as we have constructed them (Brouwer, Heyting). |
| **Formalism** | Mathematics is the manipulation of symbols according to given rules (Hilbert). |

Most working mathematicians, however, do not concern themselves with such issues. They assume that mathematics is useful for describing many (real) entities, and that it can also be developed independently of any existing reality, should one wish or need to do so.

Each of these positions has yielded interesting results. Why? Precisely because mathematics is an **object-free discipline**. Therefore, if we identify a particular class of objects, we can develop a mathematics suited to them. And surely, there are still many other interesting mathematical objects waiting to be discovered — this journey is only just beginning.

---

## 8. Mathematics as a Fine Art

Altogether, this simply means that mathematics has no privileged subject — it is, in essence, a **fine art**. Of course, it is also a science — but in a special sense. Here, an analogy with music is useful. A contemporary composer of symphonic music must, in addition to talent, possess extensive knowledge; one need only mention the study of harmony. The same is true in mathematics, especially in so-called academic mathematics. It is indeed a vast domain of knowledge — but more in the sense of something one must learn (as in music) than a science of something (as in the natural sciences).

## 9. The Experience of Mathematical Reality

One of the reasons for the relative popularity of Platonism in mathematics is likely the experience of the "physicality" of the objects studied. They cannot be arbitrarily shaped; the process of understanding them is more akin to **discovery** (of something that exists) than to construction. And this is no illusion.

Consider an example from Euclidean geometry: if the length of the base and the height of a square-based pyramid are given, then all other parameters — the angles of inclination of the faces, surface areas, volume, etc. — are thereby determined. In studying such a pyramid, we are genuinely discovering properties already given (defined), and there is no arbitrariness. Today, such problems may seem trivial — but to the first geometers, these relationships were true discoveries.

Likewise, axiomatic systems uniquely determine certain sets of possible theorems — this set is not arbitrary. If we accept a particular axiom system, then with it comes the necessity of certain relationships and the truth of particular theorems. Of course, we may adopt a different axiom system — or none at all. This is why the experience of a *sui generis* realism, or even a kind of physicality, is not uncommon in the work of a mathematician.

## 10. The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics

Why does $2 + 2 = 4$ work even in the Andromeda galaxy? Because it operates on the simplest relations — relations that are therefore **universal**. If, however, it turned out that a different arithmetic holds in some distant galaxy, that would simply mean that $2 + 2 = 4$ refers to local properties (though likely with broad applicability).

The effectiveness of mathematics is analogous to the effectiveness of drawing or literature: the world lends itself to being represented through these methods.

Mathematics is at once profoundly human and profoundly rooted in the structure of the world, because **we are rooted in that structure — and the world in ours**. In an important and essential sense, we are identical with it, and the opposition "self vs. world" or "nature vs. culture" is local and pragmatic in character.

Dichotomies such as "nature-culture" or "subjective-objective" are epistemic tools, not "ultimate truths." They serve us in specific contexts. In the natural sciences, distinguishing between "observer" and "system" helps to formulate models. In ecology, differentiating between "organism" and "environment" also has practical value. But when we begin to interpret these distinctions as radical, we start making mistakes.

## 11. Participation in the Great Creativity of the Cosmos

When the Sumerians laid out pebbles to measure area, they were not "discovering" mathematics — they were **co-creating it through interaction with matter**. Similarly, a modern mathematician defining Banach space does not invent an abstraction "out of nothing" but brings forth patterns already present in them as parts of the world. **Science belongs to nature.**

Human culture (including mathematics) is not "artificial" or "transcendent." Birds build nests, humans build group theory, and trees unfold leaves. The difference lies in complexity, not in essence. This is called **participation in the great creativity of the cosmos**.

When the opposition "I vs. the world" vanishes, so does the question: Why does mathematics fit reality? It fits — because it is part of it. It's like asking why a wave fits the ocean. Schrödinger's equation describes a wave function — but the brain that formulated it is itself a set of quantum waves. Mathematics does not describe an "external" world — it is one of its expressions.

Science and art are ways in which the world exists through us — and we through it.

---

## 12. Summary: Two Modes, One Reality

| Mode | Ars Rerum | Ars Imaginationis |
|:-----|:----------|:------------------|
| **Focus** | Fidelity to reality | Freedom of creation |
| **Method** | Observation, experiment, representation | Invention, abstraction, exploration |
| **Domain** | Natural sciences, applied mathematics | Pure mathematics, fine arts |
| **Criterion** | Truth, adequacy | Beauty, elegance, novelty |
| **Relation to world** | Conforms to reality | Explores possibility space |

The two modes are not opposed but **complementary**. Ars rerum grounds us in the structure of reality; ars imaginationis allows us to explore beyond it. Together, they constitute the full expression of human creativity — and of the world's self-expression through us.
